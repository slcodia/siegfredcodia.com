Linear Regression with Categorical Data

To keep it simple, we start with a binary categorical data or qualitative variable.

A dummy variable is a binary variable that takes values of 0 or 1. This is added to a regression model to represent factors which are binary of nature, i.e. they are either observed or not observed.

Some of the use cases are the following

  • Yes/No response
  • Categorical Value (e.g. Male vs Female)
  • Treatment Effects (control group vs treatment group)

The linear equation can be written as the following:

\[ Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_i +\beta_2\delta_i + \varepsilon_i \]

where

  • \(X_i\) is a continuous variable
  • \(\delta_i = \begin{cases} 1 & i^{th} \text{ element has the quality observed} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\)
  • \(\beta_2 :=\) effect of the dummy variable if the specified quality is possessed by element \(i\)

For our example, we want to find an equation that describes the salary of employees in Company ABC given their age and sex.

Remember that one of assumptions of regression analysis is that the error term or residual should follow a normal distribution. We perform a Shapiro-Wilk test with a null hypothesis that the residual follows a normal distribution. At \(p < 0.05\), there is sufficient evidence to conclude that residual deviates from normality.

From this, we can explore other possible models, such as including interaction effects.

Interaction Effects

  • There are times that the effect on a response variable is based on the interaction of two independent variables.
  • In the linear model without interaction effects, it is assumed that the slope of the equation is the same regardless of the value of the dummy variable; only the intercept changes, with the following case: \(intercept = \begin{cases} \beta_0+\beta_2 & \delta = 1 \\ \beta_0 & \delta =0 \end{cases}\)

We introduce in our model an interaction term that quantifies the slope of the model at different values of the categorical variable.

\[ Y_i = \beta_0+\beta_1X_i + \beta_2\delta_i + \beta_3X_i\delta_i +\varepsilon_i \]

In this model, we assume that if the value of our dummy variable \(\delta_i\) changes, the slope of the equation also varies. To summarize, the following is the slope of our model:

\[ slope = \begin{cases} \beta_1+\beta_3 & \delta = 1 \\ \beta_1 & \delta =0 \end{cases} \]

For our example, we want to answer the following specific questions:

  1. Do the employees have the same starting salaries whether they are male or female?
  2. Is the salary gap between two sexes the same across all ages? Is there a favored sex in terms of promotion opportunities?

Model 2

We need to transform first the age variable to calibrate the age range for easier interpretation, especially for the intercept, that the starting salary is received at age of 20.

Recall our model:

\[ Y_i = \beta_0+\beta_1X_i + \beta_2\delta_i + \beta_3X_i\delta_i +\varepsilon_i \]

where

  • \(Y_i\) := salary
  • \(\beta_0\) := intercept; starting salary of employee if not male, i.e. \((\delta=0)\), and if age = 20, i.e. \((X_i=0)\) .
  • \(\beta_1\) := coefficient of age_20; effect of age on salary if employee is not male, i.e. \(\delta=0\)
  • \(\beta_2\) := coefficient of sexmale; difference of starting salary of male against a female employee, i.e. intercept if \(\delta=1\)
    • \(\beta_0+\beta_2\) is the starting salary of male employees. If \(\beta_2\) is not significant, then male and female have same starting salary of \(\beta_0\)
  • \(\beta_3\) := coefficient of interaction age_20:sexmale; additional effect of age on salary if employee is male, i.e. \(\delta=1\)
    • \(\beta_1+\beta_3\) is the salary increment of male employees. If \(\beta_3\) is not significant, then the effect of age on salary is same for male and female.

In this model:

  • the coefficient of sexmale is not significant, which means that the starting salary for male and female employees are not significantly different, but;
  • the salary increment of female employees (coeffcient of age_20), and the additional salary increment of male employees (coefficient of age_20:sexmale ) are significant, which implies that the age contributes positively for both sexes, with an additional incentive for male employees.

Model 3

Since one coefficient is not significant, we regress again for another model, removing the sexmale term in the equation:

\[ Y_i = \beta_0+\beta_1X_i +\beta_3X_i\delta_i +\varepsilon_i \]

From the outputs of the regression analysis, and to answer the questions, we have the following insights:

  1. Regardless of sex, the average starting salary of employees is the same
  2. The salary gap between sexes is not constant across all ages. Annual increase also differs between the two sexes:

    • Female employees have an average annual salary increment of Php 1,936

    • Male employees have an average annual salary increment of Php 1,900 + 1,200= Php 3,100

For future studies, it is recommended to consider non-linear equations, such as using square or logarithmic transformations on some variables. Logically, the amount of salary increase an employee gets is based on the percentage of their current salary, indicating a possible exponential trend of salary given age.
